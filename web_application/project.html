Software Project

Aims:

  • Gain experience working in a small team-based environment

  • Develop an understanding of software project management

  • Learn to apply software processes in a practical setting

  • Appreciate the importance of teamwork and communication

  • Gain experience developing and working to a project plan

Due Dates:

  • Project Plan Presentation: Week 8 seminar

  • Project Plan Document: Week 8 end, Sunday, September 17, 11:59 p.m.

  • Software Product: Week 12 end, Sunday, October 22, 11:59 p.m.

Value:

  • Project Plan: 20% (Presentation 10%, Document 10%)

  • Software Product: 30% (Individual 10%, Team 20%)

Project

The software project is the main assessment component of this course. The aim is to develop and then execute a project plan for a moderately sized software project in teams of 4-5 students from the same seminar group, and then implement that plan following the Scrum process. You will need to assign Scrum roles within the project team, but all members must take on the developer role and contribute to software develpment. Students judged by the Lecturer in Charge or seminar facilitator to be insufficiently experienced to undertake the software project (e.g. who are not in their final year), will be placed in a group of similarly inexperienced students, so as not to disadvantage other groups.

The project plan will be developed in the first half of the semester, presented to the seminar group in Week 8 for feedback, then submitted as a formal document at the end of that week. You will then be held to that plan for the remainder of the project (subject to any agreed modifications). The software product will then be demonstrated in Week 13 (the last week of semester). Thus it is important to develop a realistic and reasonable plan, and then to deliver on that plan.

An important part of the project is the appropriate use of project management tools for planning and executing the project. You need to use a suitable software tool such as Pivotal Tracker, though other tools can be used — provided they support full agile planning and estimation (check with the facilitators before using any other tool). You should make use of the tool when planning your project, and be prepared to show the project plan using the tool during the plan presentation.

A list of suggested projects from previous years is given below. However, you are encouraged to choose your own project, but this must be approved by the lecturers in advance of the development of the project plan. You must ensure that the project is of a scope suitable for a team project given both the size of the team and the time available, and suitable for you to demonstrate the required proficiency in software development.

You will be guided by your seminar facilitator, who will also play the role of the client/customer of your project. Since seminar facilitators play the role of clients, they cannot provide detailed technical advice on programming languages or platforms, but will endeavour to do so to the best of their abilities. In this role, they are to be considered more as end users who talk primarily to the Product Owner. The mark for the project plan is a team mark, i.e. all members of the team receive the same mark, and is based on the presentation of an agile project plan both in a seminar and in a written document. It is advisable for the project plan to be entered into the project management tool before the plan presentation.

The marks for the software product are divided into an individual mark and a team mark. For the individual mark, you should maintain an individual diary that contains your personal record of your daily standup meetings (the entries should be written answers to the three standard questions: what did I do yesterday?, what will I do today?, is anything in my way?), plus any planning documents (such as system designs, user interface mockups). You will need to show this to your seminar facilitator at regular intervals as this contributes to your individual mark, and also so that they can assess whether your team project is on track. In addition, two seminars will be devoted to sprint reviews so that the seminar group can provide feedback to your team, plus you have the experience of participating in the audience of a sprint review. Part of your individual assessment is based on the extent to which you adopt appropriate professional and ethical practices during the project (e.g. attending meetings, keeping others informed of progress, etc.) and the use of project management tools. The team mark for the software product is based on a demonstration held in Week 13, and is partly based on the degree to which the presented product meets the system proposed in the project plan. Thus for the software product, while more sophisticated systems generally receive higher marks, it is critical to meet agreed requirements (i.e. a simpler product of sufficient scope that performs according to requirements is superior to a more complex product that fails to meet its requirements). Each member of a team generally receives the same team mark for the software product, however in "dysfunctional teams" where member contributions are highly unequal, especially taking account of software development, individual marks for this component will be adjusted to reflect the level of contribution.

Note that as a guide, it is suggested to spend roughly 40-50 hours per person on the software project over the semester (this includes all aspects of the project including planning, development, testing and demonstration). It is the responsibility of the team (at planning time) to ensure (to the best of their knowledge) that their project can be completed within such a resource allocation.

Project Plan

The overarching requirement for your project plan is to demonstrate that you can deliver a working system, subject to the given timeframe, budget and resource constraints (here the timeframe and work effort are fixed, since you have a deadline at the end of semester and you have 4-5 people to do the work). To do this, you will need to take into account how you are going to develop your system (e.g. initial product backlog of user stories and tasks, system architecture, etc.).

Remember that agile software development requires careful planning and estimation (but of a different kind from a Waterfall plan). Your project plan should contain some or all of the following (though not necessarily in this order).

  • User Stories (assigned to Value Stories or "Epics")

  • Product Backlog (prioritized and including Minimum Viable Product)

  • Estimation of Work for each Product Backlog Item

  • Release Plan (if having multiple releases)

  • Sprint Plan (for initial sprint)

  • Allocation of Roles (Scrum Master, Product Owner, Developer)

  • Meeting Schedule (Daily Standup, Sprint Planning, Sprint Review, Sprint Retrospective)

  • Relevant Extreme Programming Practices (Pair Programming, Test-Driven Development, etc.)

Software Product

As a team, you will demonstrate your product to the facilitators in the seminar in Week 13. The team mark for this component will be based on the success of the project as well as the quality of the product, in particular how well you abided by your project plan, how you responded to unforeseen events during the project, etc. In this respect, your presentation should be an honest evaluation of the outcomes of the project, like a sprint review. Your team presentation should not be a "sales pitch": your audience will play the roles of your managers, who are interested primarily in outcomes, and software engineers, who may ask detailed questions about your product's design and implementation. For the quality of the product, it is important is to produce a working system that meets the objectives and requirements stated in the project plan, and to be able to justify your design decisions. There is not necessarily any benefit to be gained by producing software that exceeds the requirements specified in the agreed plan.

Assessment

Project Plan

Marks are allocated based on the following main criteria
  • Completeness of the plan (does it clearly address all relevant aspects as described above?)
  • Feasibility of the plan (does it look to be a reasonable plan?)
It is recognized that planning is difficult and that it is virtually impossible to produce a "perfect" plan. Thus the benefit of the doubt will be given where a plan must, by nature of the topic, include uncertainty. The presentation of the plan will be assessed by the seminar facilitator and by fellow students in the seminar group.

Software Product

  • Individual mark: determined on a weekly basis (2.5% in each of weeks 9, 10, 11 and 12) though only finalized at the end of semester, based on adherence to professional and ethical practices as recorded in your individual diary, evidenced by (for example)
    • System designs, user interface mockups, etc.
    • Daily answers to the three standard questions (what did I do yesterday, what will I do today, is anything in my way)
  • Team mark: determined at the end of semester, based on the quality of the software product and teamwork practices as determined from your presentation, and subject to modification where member contributions, especially to software development, are highly unequal
    • Software quality: Requirements satisfaction, correctness, adherence to design principles
    • Teamwork practices: Appropriate refinement of scope, stories, tasks, estimations, etc., and use of project management tools

Marking Scheme (Rough guide only!)

50 Basic software system meeting a only a few objectives, with software process vaguely followed
65 Moderately advanced software system meeting major objectives, with software process more-or-less followed
75 Sophisticated, documented software system meeting most objectives, with software process followed systematically
85 Highly sophisticated, well documented software system meeting all objectives, with software process followed systematically
95 Highly ambitious, well documented software system meeting all objectives, with software process followed rigorously

Late penalty: Late submissions receive a 0 mark

Plagiarism

  • Remember that ALL work submitted for this assignment must be your own (team's) work and no code sharing or copying (between different teams) is allowed; of course, you may share code within your own team.
  • The use of third-party code or Internet sources for well-defined specific purposes is permitted, but only with clear and full documentation in your project plan and the approval of this plan by the lecturers prior to the presentation of your plan. All third-party code must be clearly identified as such in your software. The use of third-party code or Internet sources for large chunks of your project is not acceptable. See the UNSW Policy on Academic Integrity and Plagiarism for more information.
